# Valence and Emotion

## What Valence Means in Music

Valence is a measure of the musical positiveness conveyed by a track. High valence sounds happy, cheerful, and euphoric. Low valence sounds sad, depressed, or angry.

- **High valence (0.7–1.0):** Happy, euphoric, uplifting, celebratory
- **Medium valence (0.4–0.7):** Neutral, bittersweet, contemplative
- **Low valence (0.0–0.4):** Sad, melancholic, tense, dark, angry

## Valence and Emotional Regulation

Valence is the most direct musical feature for emotional matching. Research in music psychology shows:

- **Mood lifting:** High valence music (0.7+) reliably improves self-reported mood in short-term listening sessions (15–30 minutes)
- **Emotional processing:** Surprisingly, low valence music helps people process grief and sadness more effectively than forcing high valence music. Listeners feel "understood" by sad music.
- **Anxiety:** Medium-low valence (0.3–0.5) with slow tempo is more effective for anxiety than very low valence, which can deepen negative feelings

## Valence vs Mood Mismatch
A common mistake is always recommending high valence music for negative emotional states. Research shows this can feel invalidating. The better approach:
- Match valence to current state first
- Gradually shift toward target valence over a playlist
- For grief or sadness: start low valence, move to medium, then optionally high

## Valence and Genre Patterns
- Pop, funk, dance: typically high valence (0.7–0.95)
- Jazz, indie, folk: medium valence (0.5–0.75)
- Classical: wide range (0.2–0.8 depending on piece)
- Metal, dark electronic: typically low valence (0.1–0.4)
- Lofi, ambient: medium valence (0.5–0.7)
